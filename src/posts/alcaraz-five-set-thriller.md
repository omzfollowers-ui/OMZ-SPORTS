For three sets it looked as though the upset was on. Carlos Alcaraz, error-prone and visibly frustrated, trailed two sets to one and faced a break point early in the fourth that would have left him a game from the exit. What followed was the kind of comeback that defines careers.

The turn came not from a single shot but from a decision: shorter points, earlier aggression, a willingness to take the ball on the rise and accept the misses that came with it. The fourth set evened the match. The fifth, played in front of a crowd that had long since abandoned any pretence of neutrality, was a procession of breaks and counter-breaks until Alcaraz finally held his nerve to serve it out.

Four hours and 36 minutes. By the end both players were cramping, and the handshake at the net carried the weight of mutual respect that only that kind of contest produces.

"I don't know how I won that," Alcaraz said, grinning, towel around his neck. "I was playing badly and he was playing unbelievable. Sometimes you just have to keep finding a way." He will need to find a few more on Sunday, against an opponent who has dropped only one set all tournament.
