Carlos Alcaraz won Roland Garros for the second time on Sunday afternoon and did it the hard way — five sets against a Sinner who played some of the finest tennis of his career and still came up short. The final score was 6-3, 4-6, 6-4, 3-6, 6-2, and the numbers do not quite capture what the match felt like to watch.

The third set was tennis at a level that does not often exist. Both players hitting through each other's defences, both covering the court with an athleticism that had Court Philippe-Chatrier on its feet for extended stretches. Alcaraz won it on a single break, converted after six opportunities. Sinner had six of his own and converted none.

That moment — the break, the fist pump that followed, the shift in body language — was where the match turned. Sinner levelled in the fourth but the fifth was Alcaraz's from the first point. There is something in his ability to raise his level inside decisive sets that no statistical model has yet found a satisfying explanation for. He simply becomes better when it matters more.

At 23, Alcaraz now has four Grand Slam titles. The comparison to Djokovic, Federer, and Nadal at the same age — which used to feel presumptuous — is now the obvious one to make. Two of those three are long retired.

The clay season is over. Wimbledon begins in three weeks. The question of which surface he is most dangerous on no longer has a clear answer.
