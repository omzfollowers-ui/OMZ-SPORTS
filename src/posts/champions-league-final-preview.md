Saturday's Champions League final pits Real Madrid against Inter Milan in what neutral observers and analysts alike are calling the most evenly matched final in a decade. The case for either side winning it is compelling; the case for it going to extra time is stronger still.

Madrid arrive as serial winners of this competition, but this squad carries less of the institutional knowledge that defined their run of dominance earlier in the decade. Several of the players who delivered those nights are gone, replaced by younger, more explosive talent that has been brilliant in bursts and inconsistent over full 90-minute periods at the highest level.

Inter's strength is system. Simone Inzaghi has had four years to embed a structure that presses high but transitions vertically, denying opponents time to build and exploiting space behind the line with a speed that has punished every side they have faced in this competition. Their defensive record — six clean sheets in eleven knockout games — is the tournament's best.

The key duel will be between Madrid's Bellingham, operating from the left of a midfield three, and Inter's double pivot of Barella and Calhanoglu. If Madrid can isolate Bellingham in space, they have a creator capable of unlocking any defence. If Inter can crowd that zone, they can neutralise the threat and make it a game decided by set pieces — where they hold the advantage.

Kick off is 8pm local time. The weather forecast for Munich is clear. Every seat in the Allianz Arena has been claimed since March.
