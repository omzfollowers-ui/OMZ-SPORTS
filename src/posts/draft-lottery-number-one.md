The ping-pong balls do not care about your rebuild timeline, your fanbase's patience, or the carefully leaked tank strategy that was supposed to guarantee a top-three finish. On Tuesday night they delivered the kind of result the lottery exists to produce: a team sitting fifth in the odds vaulted four places to land the number one pick.

For a franchise that has not picked first since the previous century, the timing could not be better. This draft is headlined by a 19-year-old big man scouts have compared, cautiously, to the best two-way centres of the modern era — a player who can protect the rim, switch onto guards, and stretch the floor to the arc. In a league that has spent a decade insisting the traditional big was dead, he is the argument for its revival.

The teams that fell in the order will spin it as opportunity. There is genuine depth at the top of this class, and the second and third picks are far from consolation prizes. But everyone in the building knew which name the commissioner was about to read, and which front office would be celebrating. Sometimes the lottery is just luck. This year, for one team, it was everything.
