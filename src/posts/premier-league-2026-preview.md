Every summer produces a consensus favourite and every summer the Premier League finds a way to complicate the narrative. This year feels different. Liverpool have signed Florian Wirtz for a British record fee, retained every key player from a side that won the title by eight points last season, and added depth in positions that were considered their only vulnerability. The case for anyone else is not impossible — it is just thin.

Manchester City begin the season under scrutiny on two fronts. Their financial fair play case continues to occupy lawyers and journalists in roughly equal measure, and on the pitch, the post-Haaland transition is not yet resolved. They have a squad of exceptional players managed by a coach who has reinvented his system before. They will be competitive. The question is whether competitive is enough.

Arsenal's sixth-place finish last season shocked the club's own supporters, who had spent two years believing a title was imminent. The response in the transfer window has been pointed — three new signings, all in their early twenties, all positioned to compete immediately for starting roles. Mikel Arteta's faith in process over results has been tested; the board's faith in Arteta appears undiminished.

Chelsea's summer is best described as characteristically complicated. Four signings, two departures that surprised the dressing room, and a manager who has been given assurances of stability that the club's recent history does not quite support.

The opening weekend fixtures are released Friday. The season begins August 15.
