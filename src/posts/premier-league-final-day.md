It has come down to this. After 37 rounds, three clubs remain mathematically alive for the title, separated by two points, and every plausible outcome will be decided across a single afternoon of simultaneous kick-offs.

The leaders control their own fate: win, and the trophy is theirs regardless of events elsewhere. But their final fixture is away at a side scrapping for a European place, hardly the gentle send-off a champion-elect would choose. A draw opens the door. A defeat throws it wide.

Behind them, the chasing pair must win and hope. One has the superior goal difference and a home tie against a team already on the beach; the other faces a rival with nothing to play for but pride and the chance to spoil a neighbour's party — historically a powerful motivator.

Managers spent the week downplaying the maths and talking about "controlling the controllables," that weary phrase that means nothing and everything. The players know better. So do the supporters, who will spend Sunday afternoon watching one match while frantically refreshing scores from another. This is the league at its cruel, glorious best.
