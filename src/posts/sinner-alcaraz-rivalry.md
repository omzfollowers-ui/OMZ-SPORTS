Every era of tennis gets the rivalry it needs. For nearly two decades the sport was carried by three players who divided the major titles among themselves and left scraps for everyone else. That era has closed. The one replacing it has a clear centre of gravity: Jannik Sinner and Carlos Alcaraz, separated by a sliver of ranking points and a chasm of style.

Sinner is precision incarnate — flat, early, relentless, a metronome who turns the baseline into a place opponents go to be slowly outlasted. Alcaraz is chaos with a plan, a shotmaker who treats the drop shot as a first option and the highlight reel as a tactic. Watching them play each other is like watching two different answers to the same question, neither willing to concede the other is correct.

The numbers are still close to even, which is precisely why it grips. There is no settled hierarchy, no clear favourite walking onto the court, only the sense that whoever wins will have earned it against the one player capable of pushing them to their limit.

They are 22 and 23. If the bodies hold and the hunger stays, this is the matchup that will define the next ten years of majors. The sport could not have scripted it better.
