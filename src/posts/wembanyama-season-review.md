When the San Antonio Spurs selected Victor Wembanyama first overall in 2023, the conversation was mostly about potential. Three seasons later, the conversation has shifted. The potential is no longer the story. The performance is.

Wembanyama averaged 28.4 points, 13.1 rebounds, 4.9 assists, and 4.3 blocks this season — a statistical combination that has no precedent in NBA history. More importantly, the Spurs won 54 games, finished second in the Western Conference, and made the second round of the playoffs for the first time since 2019.

What separates this season from the first two is not the numbers — those have always been remarkable. It is the consistency. The player who looked physically fragile in his debut year, who missed 23 games in year two, has played in 79 of 82 games this season and maintained his level throughout. His body has changed visibly; the strength coaches in San Antonio have done quiet, consequential work.

Head coach Gregg Popovich, making what he has suggested will be his final season on the bench, called Wembanyama the most complete player he has coached. For a man who coached Tim Duncan for two decades, that is not a sentence he would use carelessly.

The Spurs are no longer rebuilding. They are built. What they do with that over the next five years will define the league's competitive landscape.
