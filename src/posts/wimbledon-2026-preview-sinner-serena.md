LONDON — The lawns are immaculate. The strawberries are ready. And tomorrow morning, the gates of the All England Club swing open for Wimbledon 2026 — and few editions of The Championships have arrived carrying quite this much storyline.

The headline dominating the build-up is the one that is not happening: Carlos Alcaraz will not be at SW19. The Spaniard, who lit up these courts with such brilliance in 2023 and 2024, has withdrawn with a wrist injury, leaving a cavernous hole in the men's draw. That means defending champion Jannik Sinner enters the fortnight as the undisputed favourite, though he carries the added pressure of a surprise second-round exit at Roland-Garros.

"I feel ready. I've been preparing specifically for this," Sinner said at his pre-tournament press conference on Saturday.

But the story that has truly gripped the tennis world is the return of Serena Williams. At 44 years old, nearly four years removed from professional competition, the most decorated player in women's tennis history has walked back through the gates of the place she once ruled with an iron fist. She will play both singles and doubles. The world will be watching.

In the women's draw, Aryna Sabalenka is the top seed while defending champion Iga Swiatek targets back-to-back Wimbledon titles — a feat no player has managed since Serena herself. Elena Rybakina, Jessica Pegula, and a resurgent Coco Gauff complete a field that feels as deep and dangerous as any in recent memory.

Wimbledon begins Monday. It already feels like something special.
