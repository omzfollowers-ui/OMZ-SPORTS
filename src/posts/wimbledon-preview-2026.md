Wimbledon begins Monday and the absence of a dominant favourite makes this one of the most genuinely open Championships in memory. In previous years, the question was always who would stop Djokovic or Williams — now the question is simply who wants it enough.

In the men's draw, Carlos Alcaraz is seeded first but arrives with only five grass-court matches this season after an elbow injury kept him off the surface until the second week of Queen's Club. He reached the semi-final there, which was encouraging, but his movement — the defining element of his game on every other surface — looked tentative on the occasions he changed direction sharply. He is the favourite because of ability, not because of form.

Jannik Sinner, the world number two, has done more on grass this summer than in any previous year. He won Halle without dropping a set, and his serve-and-forehand combination suits the surface better than his ranking and reputation on clay would suggest. If his hip holds up through two weeks of sliding and stretching, he is the man most likely to hold the trophy on Sunday.

The dark horse most in the draw are discussing is Tommy Paul. The American, now 29, has spent two seasons reconfiguring his game around the net — and on grass that investment pays dividends. He reached the semi-final here last year.

The women's draw is equally open. Aryna Sabalenka, who has never won Wimbledon, arrives as top seed and with a motivation that last year's final defeat has sharpened considerably.
