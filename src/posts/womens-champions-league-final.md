Saturday's final in Lisbon pits the tournament's most ruthless attack against its meanest defence, the kind of stylistic clash that tends to produce either a classic or a war of attrition. There is rarely much in between.

One side has scored in every knockout match, built around a front three whose movement has tormented even well-drilled back lines. They press high, commit numbers forward, and dare opponents to play through them. It has been thrilling to watch and, occasionally, nerve-shredding to support.

Their opponents have taken the opposite path: organised, patient, and almost impossible to break down, conceding just twice in the entire competition. Their game plan will be familiar — absorb, frustrate, and strike on the transitions that the favourites' adventurous shape inevitably leaves available.

The women's game has spent the last decade growing into exactly this kind of occasion: a sold-out stadium, a global broadcast audience, and two teams good enough to justify both. Whoever lifts the trophy will have earned it. The neutral, for once, simply gets to enjoy it.
